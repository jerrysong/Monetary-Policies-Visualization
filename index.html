Preface

A Fairly Real Tale: America in the Time of Prosperity

Once upon a time

The Economy in America was booming. Fueled by lax mortgage standards, the housing market soared and spending was plentiful.

And everyday

the seers proclaimed throughout the land that the housing market could never fall.

Until one day

Toxic mortgages brought one of the largest financial firms in the country to its knees. The Economy was beset by a plague of darkness as the Great Recession arrived.

At the magical halls of the Federal Reserve

The twelve regional Presidents of the Federal Reserve were devising a plan! They would cast their new spell, Quantative Easing!

For the first time

The spell worked its magic and the Economy stabilized. But the spell made the wizards very hungry, and they gobbled up tons of government bonds and mortgage-backed-securities.

But within a year the spell wore off

So the Fed had to cast it once again! It didn’t work well enough, and so the Fed cast it once again! All the while, they were gobbling up more and more assets to fuel their spellcasting.

And finally

The Fed hoped their work had been enough, but in their magical endeavors, they found that they had eaten $3.5 trillion dollars of assets. That was a lot to stomach, but they hoped this got the Economy back on its feet.

Chapter I

The Federal Reserve: The Literal Moneymaker

"I don't want to go from Wild Turkey to 'cold turkey' overnight"
-- Richard Fisher, Former Dallas Federal Reserve President regading the timing of major policy shift

$3.5 Trillion Dollars. That's enough to give every adult in the the U.S. almost $15,000, or enough to to pay off every student loan, car loan and credit card in America. That’s how much money the Federal Reserve effectively attempted to push into the economy in hopes of giving it a jump-start in the aftermath of the Great Recession.

The Federal Reserve is comprised of 12 regional Presidents and is in charge of monetary policy- which is effectively the creation of money. The Fed's stated mandate is to balance unemployment and inflation.

Historically, the Federal Reserve primarily acted by changing interest rates, but after slashing the interest rate to near 0% in late 2008, the Federal Reserve decided that it was time for a more drastic measure: Quantitative Easing (QE). The Fed starting buying tens of billions of mortgage backed securities. When the first round didn’t produce satisfactory long-term results, the Fed tried two more rounds.

Chapter II

Stocks and Bonds: A Pack of Feral Hogs?

"I don’t think anyone can break the Fed... but I do believe that big money does organize itself somewhat like feral hogs. If they detect a weakness or a bad scent, they’ll go after it.”
-- Richard Fisher, Former Dallas Federal Reserve President regading traders in the stock market

One of the reasons the Federal Reserve went to such drastic measures was in hopes of stabilizing flailing financial markets in a manner that would create a “wealth effect”, where increasing financials markets lead to higher consumer confidence, which leads to more spending. This concept of a “wealth effect” is a similar to the “trickle-down theory”, where giving money to people who are well off leads to more investment than ends up helping everyone.

Certainly, the combination of “easy money” policies (such as QE and dropping the interest rate to 0%) helped the stock market recover from its Great Recession lows. However, it’s questionable whether that was fully positive. After all, shouldn’t increase in stock prices be driven by actual economic fundamentals?

Chapter 3

Everyday Americans

"It's the Economy, Stupid"
-- Ancient American Proverb (James Carville, repeatedly during Bill Clinton's 1992 Presidential Campaign

So, while the actions of the Federal Reserve drove stock and bond markets for two years, how well did their actions fulfill the Fed mandates of balancing inflation and unemployment? And what do those mandates really mean in practice? Effectively, the Fed works to ensure that prices aren’t rising too quickly (i.e. inflation) and that money is spread out in a healthy manner through society (unemployment rate and wage levels).

How well did Quantitative Easing help the Federal Reserve meet its mandates? The Fed's stated goals are 5% unemployment and 2% inflation, which they are close to. Of course, their inflation metric ignores housing costs in a time when affordable housing crises are prevalent in many major cities and their unemployment metric counts someone working 1 hour a week as fully employed; taking in underemployment, the unemployment rate jumps up to 8.6%. Additionally, studies have shown that since 2007, about an additional 1% of the adult population who want a job stopped looking for work (See Summary for more information on Labor Participation Rates).

These metrics may not really provide a wholly accurate picture about how people are faring. However, we can look at how income for a variety of Americans has changed in comparison to changes in costs. This will allow us to see the financial status of many Americans has evolved post Great-Recession.

Chapter 4

Debt: How do people cope when their costs exceed their income? Borrow more!

Post Great Recession, student loan debt has skyrocketed and auto debt is not far behind. Meanwhile, despite increasing house prices, mortgage debt has relatively flat, indicating that cash has been a major driver in real estate prices. This in turns means that lower income citizens may find it increasingly difficult to buy their own home, which has been a traditional method of increasing wealth in the United States.

Overall debt has increased a reasonable 12% since 2008, but non-mortgage debt has spiked by 47%. Mortgage debt- which has only recently surpassed its' beginning 2007 levels- does dwarf all other types of debt combined (it's about twice the sum of all other debt types), which makes sense given how expensive buying and owning a home is and the innate value of a home. Student loans and sub-prime auto loans have surged; student loans were never even impacted by the recession.

Summary

The Federal Reserve took drastic measures after the Great Recession in hopes of stimulating the economy. They purchased $3.5 trillion in financial products over several years. The Fed's actions helped the stock market recover and reach new heights, but didn't help bring that same prosperity to most Americans. In fact, Americans with the lowest income are in many regards still recovering from the Great Recession. Looking forward, the Federal Reserve is looking to start slowly unwinding their balance sheet. This could effectively reverse some of the effects on the Stock Market caused by Quantative Easing. However, the Fed is concerned that inflation does not seem to be picking up, and therefore may hold off on their unwinding.
